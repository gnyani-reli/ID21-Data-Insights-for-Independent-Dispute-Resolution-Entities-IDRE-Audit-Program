Are certain providers filing too many disputes on the same code — and asking far more than their peers?

Two patterns stood out in the public dispute data. First, some providers are filing a disproportionate number of disputes for a single procedure code in a single region — far more than any of their peers. Second, those same providers are often requesting payment amounts that dwarf what other providers ask for the exact same service. When both patterns show up together, and the provider keeps winning, that combination is worth a closer look.

This page walks through the analysis step by step — first the volume screen, then the price comparison. The 25 records in the final table are flagged for follow-up review, not accused of wrongdoing. Case files would need to be pulled before any conclusions are drawn.

Step 1 — How the population narrows before price is considered
Starting with 195,870 combinations, five successive screens narrow to 25
Each row is a screening gate applied in order. Dollar amounts are not part of this step — this is purely about dispute volume behavior.
We started with every unique combination of provider, procedure code, and region in the data. Most providers file a handful of disputes per code and look completely normal. We progressively filter out the ordinary ones: first removing combinations with too few disputes to be statistically meaningful, then those with too few peer providers to compare against, and finally narrowing to only those where one provider's share of disputes stands out as highly concentrated. By the end, 25 combinations survive and move to Step 2.
Filing a lot of disputes isn't automatically a red flag. What we're looking for is a provider filing a disproportionate share of all disputes for one particular code in one region versus every peer nearby. By keeping price out of this step, the two concerns (volume concentration and pricing behavior) can be weighed separately.

Several of these providers are requesting 5×, 6×, even 9× what their peers ask for the exact same ED visit code — and winning 88–94% of the time. A handful share billing domains, suggesting they may be part of the same network or managed by the same billing operation.
